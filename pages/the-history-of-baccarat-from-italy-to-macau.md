### Origins in Italy

The earliest forms of baccarat are believed to have originated in Italy during the late 15th century. The game is frequently attributed to an Italian named Felix Falguiere, who initially named it “baccara,” meaning zero in Italian. This name was fitting given that tens and face cards, which are typically high-value cards in other games, were worth zero points in baccarat.

The Italian version of baccarat then branched into two distinct games: "baccarat banque" and "chemin de fer." These early iterations were primarily enjoyed by the aristocracy and have more complex rules involving a banker.

### Spread to France

Baccarat crossed the border into France in the late 15th century and became a favorite pastime among the French nobility. It was during the reign of King Charles VIII that baccarat gained noticeable popularity. The game's version known as "chemin de fer," which means railway in French, became especially well-liked. In this variant, players take turns being the banker, and its name purportedly derives from the speed of the French railways, possibly alluding to the game's dynamic nature relative to its predecessors.

The French nobility fostered a sense of exclusivity around baccarat. It remained a rarefied activity, primarily reserved for the wealthy and well-connected.

### Development in Great Britain and the Americas

By the 19th century, baccarat expanded beyond the bounds of Europe, reaching Great Britain, and then the Americas. The British version of the game evolved into "punto banco," which was later transported to Argentina and eventually to the United States.

In America, punto banco underwent several adaptations to cater to casino settings, emphasizing its appeal as a game of chance rather than skill. This version dispensed with the need for players to make choices during the game, making it accessible to a broader audience.

### Rise in Popularity in Asia

The most significant development in baccarat history occurred in the 20th century with its introduction to the gambling mecca of Macau. Colonial influences contributed to its integration into Chinese culture, where baccarat was adapted and embraced on a major scale. By the 1960s, gambling magnate Stanley Ho helped establish baccarat as a central attraction in Macau's casinos, which eventually transformed the city into the “Las Vegas of the East.” 

Macau is now synonymous with high-stakes baccarat, often referred to as the "baccarat capital of the world." This region accounts for a substantial portion of global baccarat revenue, largely driven by Asian players who frequent Macau's opulent casinos.
